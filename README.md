The deployment of High Performance Computing (HPC) facilities driven by Artificial Intelligence (AI) and rising computing 
demands across diverse regions requires a shift from traditional thermal monitoring to a multiphysics framework that accounts 
for atmospheric pressure, temperature, and chemical stressors. This study presents a simulation approach that combines modeling 
and monitoring data to evaluate how local environments affect hardware survival and sustainability. Analyzing three zones in Latin America 
and the Caribbean: Cartagena de Indias (coastal/saline), Bucaramanga (mid-altitude/stable) in Colombia, and Toluca (high-altitude/low-pressure) 
in Mexico, reveals an 'Altitude-Temperature Paradox,' where thin high-altitude air accelerates hardware aging more than sea-level heat. 
By linking physical degradation with $CO_2e$ impact, we show that sustainability depends on geographical balance.
